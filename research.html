Trust, Credibility, and Decision-Making in High-Stakes Information Environments

My research asks how people decide what to trust when information is uncertain, consequential, and mediated by platforms, creators, algorithms, or AI systems.

My doctoral research studied why people trust severe weather livestreamers during moments of real risk, and what that reveals about the future of AI-mediated information, crisis communication, and public trust.

Severe weather as a signature case

Severe weather livestreams are a high-stakes case of platform-mediated expertise. Audiences are not just watching content; they are monitoring how a host reasons, interprets official data, handles uncertainty, and responds to changing risk in real time. Livestream chat functions as a site of collective verification, where viewers cross-check claims, share radar readings, and challenge or corroborate the host's interpretation as it happens.

Advised by Mark Zachry and David W. McDonald (co-chairs), with committee member Kate Starbird.

Why this matters beyond weather

Entertainment platforms are increasingly functioning as emergency information infrastructure, whether or not they were designed to. The same trust mechanisms that let audiences calibrate confidence in a weatherfluencer apply anywhere institutional and platform-native sources of expertise compete for the same audience under time pressure.

AI-generated content and synthetic media

As AI-generated content enters product marketing, public information, and crisis communication, the same questions recur: when does synthesized or AI-authored material earn trust, when does it require provenance or disclosure, and what happens to public sensemaking when the source of expertise is no longer legibly human? I extend the dissertation's framework into this territory directly.

Platform-mediated expertise

Two theoretical contributions anchor this work. Inspectable interpretive work describes the public visibility of a host's reasoning as it unfolds in real time, enabling audiences to calibrate trust without relying on institutional proxies. Recursive sensemaking describes how audiences don't just evaluate the content of a stream; they simultaneously assess the host's own fitness and legitimacy to host, live, as events unfold.

Trust & safety and public sensemaking

Trust, following Möllering, is a continuously enacted suspension of doubt, renewed or disrupted through each successive interaction, distinct from trustworthiness as a settled evaluative judgment (Mayer). That distinction has direct implications for platform governance: credibility online is rarely a fixed property of a source, it's an ongoing, collective accomplishment that platforms can support or undermine by design.

Where this agenda goes next

The mechanisms that let audiences calibrate trust in a human weatherfluencer, in real time, under uncertainty, are the same mechanisms that will matter as AI-generated content enters crisis communication, product marketing, and public information systems. That bridge, from platform-mediated expertise to AI-mediated trust, is the core of my current research and industry work.

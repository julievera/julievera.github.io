Trust, Credibility, and Decision-Making in High-Stakes Information Environments

My research asks how people decide what to trust when information is uncertain, consequential, and mediated by platforms, creators, algorithms, or AI systems.

My doctoral research studied why people trust severe weather livestreamers during moments of real risk, and what that reveals about the future of AI-mediated information, crisis communication, and public trust.

Why weather?

Severe weather livestreams are a high-stakes case of platform-mediated expertise. Audiences are not just watching content; they are monitoring how a host reasons, interprets official data, handles uncertainty, and responds to changing risk in real time.

Why this matters beyond weather

The same trust mechanisms matter as AI-generated content enters product marketing, public information, crisis communication, and synthetic media environments.

Advised by Mark Zachry and David W. McDonald (co-chairs), with committee member Kate Starbird.

Key Concepts

Inspectable interpretive work The public visibility of a host's reasoning as it unfolds in real time, enabling audiences to calibrate trust without relying on institutional proxies.
Trust as the management of reliance Trust, following Möllering, as a continuously enacted suspension of doubt, renewed or disrupted through each successive interaction, distinct from trustworthiness as a settled evaluative judgment (Mayer).
Recursive sensemaking Audiences don't just evaluate the content of a weatherfluencer's stream; they simultaneously assess the host's own fitness and legitimacy to host, live, as events unfold.
Implications for AI-mediated crisis content As institutional and algorithmic information sources multiply, the mechanisms publics use to calibrate trust in real time become directly relevant to synthetic and AI-generated crisis content.

Extending the agenda

The mechanisms that let audiences calibrate trust in a human weatherfluencer, in real time, under uncertainty, are the same mechanisms that will matter as AI-generated content enters crisis communication, product marketing, and public information systems. That bridge, from platform-mediated expertise to AI-mediated trust, is the core of my current research and industry work.

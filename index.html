Authority · Interpretation · Collective Sensemaking

Julie A. Vera, Ph.D.

Authority, interpretation, and collective sensemaking in mediated information environments.

I study how people decide whose interpretations to rely on when information is unfolding, consequential, and mediated, whether it’s mediated by platforms or AI systems. My work examines how authority becomes recognizable and usable outside traditional institutional structures, from severe weather livestreams and creator-led crisis information to AI-generated content, marketplaces, and other systems where people must act before certainty is possible.

Current directions

Extending weatherfluencer research into questions of authority, community, and recurrence.

I’m extending my dissertation work on weatherfluencers into longer-term questions about how platform-native authority develops across events, seasons, and communities.

This includes how repeated exposure, visible interpretive performance, audience return, local observation, ground-truth reporting, and community infrastructure shape who people come to rely on during severe weather.

The broader question remains: how do people decide whose interpretations to rely on when information is unfolding, consequential, and mediated, whether by platforms, communities, institutions, or AI systems?

Where this work travels

My research starts from severe weather livestreams, but the underlying problem appears anywhere people must decide what to rely on before certainty is possible.

I apply this lens across product, platform, and organizational contexts where authority, evidence, credibility, and interpretation shape what people are willing to do.

AI-generated content, evidence, and reliance When does AI-authored content become usable? When does it require proof, provenance, or human accountability? What kinds of claims exceed the authority users are willing to grant to generated summaries?
Credibility and choice under information asymmetry How do people decide what to rely on in marketplaces where information is incomplete, incentives are uneven, and interfaces must make credibility legible?
Research infrastructure for ambiguous product spaces How do teams build shared vocabulary, evidence, and decision criteria before ambiguity hardens into roadmap commitments?
Search, relevance, and interpretive support How do people use search, filters, ranking, recommendations, and content cues to decide what deserves attention, comparison, or action?

Research

Authority under uncertainty

My dissertation examines severe weather livestreams as a high-stakes case of platform-mediated expertise: how publics make interpretations usable when a creator’s professional standing remains unsettled.

How do people decide whose interpretations to rely on when information is unfolding, consequential, and mediated, whether by platforms or AI systems?

The work develops concepts for understanding how authority is constructed and sustained in mediated environments, including inspectable interpretive work, audience-constituted authority, interactional trust work, and collective sensemaking at the fringe of formal institutions.

Inspectable interpretive work How creators make reasoning visible enough for audiences to inspect, question, corroborate, or provisionally rely on.
Audience-constituted authority How repeated uptake, questioning, comparison, reporting, and return across events make a creator usable as an interpreter.
Interactional trust work How chat participants monitor, negotiate, and coordinate reliance in real time.
Collective sensemaking at the fringe of authority How publics make unfolding events actionable even when formal standing does not settle who should be relied upon.
